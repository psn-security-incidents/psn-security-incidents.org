$An Open Letter to US PSN Executives

To:

The Management Team of Sony USA — PlayStation Network Division

February 2026 — This is a placeholder open letter. The final version will incorporate additional evidence and community input.

This open letter is a placeholder. Its final form will be shaped by the evidence gathered from affected account holders, documented support interactions, and publicly reported incidents. What follows is the core ask.

The Problem

'High-value' PSN accounts — those created at the inception of the network, or 'High-profile' PSN accounts; those with a public persona such as trophy hunters or top players are being stolen through methods that bypass two-factor authentication, passkeys, and all security measure available to account holders. In some cases the attacks appear to be carried out via social engineering of Sony’s own customer support agents and those have been the most difficult to reverse.

When victims contact support to recover their accounts, they may be told that the account has been “protected,” from modification. if an account is successfully reverted after a hack, the hacker can trigger a revenge ban or suspension: A no-appeal state applied as a consequence of actions taken by the hacker, not the owner. Flags like these effectively lock the legitimate owner out from restoration, or permanently ban the account because the appeal process does not look at the reasons for the detected violations.

The Ask

At a bare minimum, PSN should offer a dedicated case resolution point of contact for victims who are willing and able to demonstrate continuous prior ownership of an account that has been stolen through little to no fault of their own.

This resolution process should:

  • Restore accounts upon verified ownership — using identity verification methods that Sony already possesses (IP address logs, console serial numbers, credit card details, transaction history, original email, original PSN ID) to confirm the claimant is the legitimate account holder.
  • Erase or mask all Terms of Service violations that occurred during the period the account was out of the owner’s hands. A victim of a hack should be punished for actions taken by the person who stole their account.
  • Not reject support requests on the basis that an account is “protected,” “banned,” “suspended,” or in any other state where those flags were applied as a direct result of the hack. These states are symptoms of the theft, not disqualifying conditions.

In summary, create an executive escalation path for the rare but difficult cases where existing Sony support channels are blocked from assisting victims by the very flags that the hack itself created. Frontline agents are not at fault — they are following policy. But that policy currently serves to block communication by victimas.
